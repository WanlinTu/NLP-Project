2. You cite roughly 2,500 filings, but the project spec explicitly says "10-K, 10-Q, **8-K**." Why did you exclude 8-Ks? That is a significant omission — 8-Ks contain material event disclosures (earnings surprises, M&A announcements, guidance revisions) that often move stocks more than quarterly MD&A narratives. Applying our factor extraction framework to 8-Ks would require a fundamentally different parsing and question-routing approach. We acknowledge this leaves signal on the table — 8-Ks capture M&A, guidance changes, and material events that move stocks. A future extension could build an 8-K-specific extraction module, but that is a separate pipeline, not a missing step in this one.

Our signal is designed for systematic portfolio construction, not event-driven trading. The +3.69% VP excess return over 21 days suggests the market does not fully price in the qualitative content we extract, at least in the tails.

### Slide 4 — Project Overview

6. The spec says the RL alignment goal is to ensure "the distribution of sentiment signals linearly align with each sentiment cohort" for a chosen time horizon. You have chosen 21-day excess returns. Why 21 days specifically? Is there a financial rationale, or is this inherited from the sample code? Did you test 5-day, 10-day, or 63-day windows?

> The 21-day window was inherited from the sample code and the provided returns data (filing_returns_1m.csv contains ret_21d). There is a financial rationale: 21 trading days is approximately one calendar month, which is long enough for the market to digest filing content but short enough to isolate the filing's impact from subsequent events. We did not test alternative windows for the midterm. Task 3 (GRPO) explicitly plans to optimize across 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month horizons, which will allow us to evaluate whether the signal is stronger at longer holding periods where fundamental information may take more time to be reflected in prices.

7. You mention Sharpe ratio, information ratio, and alpha as success metrics. Can you define each precisely? What is the denominator in your information ratio — tracking error against SPY or against the sector?

> Sharpe ratio = (annualized mean excess return of the long-short portfolio) / (annualized standard deviation of those returns). We compute monthly L/S returns and annualize: Sharpe = (mean_monthly / std_monthly) * sqrt(12). Information ratio (IR) = mean active return / tracking error, where active return is the portfolio return minus the benchmark (SPY) return, and tracking error is the standard deviation of those active returns. Our IR is computed against SPY, not the sector ETF. Alpha is the intercept from regressing portfolio excess returns on SPY excess returns — the return unexplained by market exposure. We acknowledge that using XLI (industrials ETF) as the benchmark would be more precise for isolating sector-neutral alpha, and plan to add that comparison in the final submission.

13. You use "TOC anchor matching" to locate MD&A sections. What happens when a filing does not have a table of contents, or when the TOC anchor IDs are inconsistent? Did you manually verify extraction accuracy on a random sample?

> When a filing lacks a TOC or has inconsistent anchor IDs, our parser falls back to regex-based section header matching — searching for "Item 7" or "Item 2" text patterns directly in the HTML body. This fallback handles most edge cases but is less reliable, which accounts for some of the <5% failure rate. We did manually verify extraction accuracy on a random sample of approximately 30 filings across different tickers and time periods, confirming that the extracted text corresponded to the actual MD&A section. The main failure mode is over-extraction (capturing text beyond the MD&A boundary) rather than under-extraction, which is less harmful since the downstream question routing filters irrelevant content.

14. The 14 question categories include 3 sector-specific ones (Airlines, Defense, Industrial). How do you route questions — does every filing get all 60 questions, or only the relevant subset? If routing is keyword-based, what is the false-positive rate? Could a filing about "defense spending" in a general industrial company trigger defense-specific questions?

> Routing is two-tiered. First, sector-specific questions (airlines_transport, defense_government, industrial_infrastructure) are only sent to filings from tickers in that sub-sector, based on our ticker_mapping.json classification. A general industrial company will never receive defense-specific questions regardless of its text content. Second, within the applicable question set, our question_router.py uses keyword matching to map subsection titles to relevant question categories. If routing yields fewer than 10 matched questions for a subsection, it falls back to sending all applicable questions. The false-positive rate on keyword routing is non-trivial but bounded — a subsection about "supply chain defense" in a general industrial company would match supply_chain_operations keywords, not defense_government keywords, because the routing operates on the ticker's sector assignment first.

15. Each filing yields "25 to 30 factors." But you have 67,741 factors across 2,542 filings — that is an average of 26.6 factors per filing. What is the variance? 16. The 6,000-token batch size for chunk Q&A — how was that number chosen? What happens at chunk boundaries — can a factor that spans two chunks get split and double-counted, or lost entirely?

> The 6,000-token batch size was chosen empirically to fit within the model's context window (8,192 tokens for DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B) while leaving room for the question prompt, system instructions, and the model's chain-of-thought response. Chunks are split along subsection boundaries (bold headings in MD&A), not at arbitrary token positions, so the risk of splitting a coherent discussion is reduced. However, a factor theme that spans two subsections (e.g., margin discussion that starts in one heading and continues in the next) can produce duplicate factors. This is handled in the synthesis step: after chunk-level Q&A, the model synthesizes all chunk-level answers for each question into a single consolidated factor per filing. Duplicate or overlapping mentions are merged during synthesis. Some residual duplication may persist, but the synthesis step is specifically designed to address this.

### Slide 8 — Top Factors by Significance

22. You rank factors by |IC| times coverage. Walk me through the math: IC is the Spearman rank correlation between what exactly — the numeric sentiment score (-2 to +2) for that specific factor key and the filing's 21-day excess return? Computed across all filings where that factor appears?

> Correct. For each unique factor key (e.g., "cost_margins.input_costs"), we collect all filings where that factor appears. Each observation is a pair: (sentiment score mapped to numeric: very_negative=-2, negative=-1, neutral=0, positive=+1, very_positive=+2) and (21-day excess return = stock return - SPY return over the 21 trading days following the filing date). IC is the Spearman rank correlation across all such pairs for that factor key. The significance metric is |IC| * coverage, where coverage is the number of filings containing that factor. This rewards factors that are both predictive (high |IC|) and broadly observed (high coverage). A factor with IC = 0.5 but n = 12 scores lower than a factor with IC = 0.10 and n = 1,195, reflecting the trade-off between effect size and statistical reliability.

23. The "reputation_risk" factor has IC = 0.512, which is extraordinarily high — institutional quant funds would pay billions for a 0.5 IC signal. But n=12. With 12 observations, what is the standard error on that Spearman correlation? Is it even remotely significant? Why is it displayed so prominently?

> The standard error on a Spearman correlation with n=12 is approximately 1/sqrt(n-3) = 1/sqrt(9) = 0.33. So the 95% confidence interval for IC = 0.512 is roughly [-0.15, +1.0] — it is not statistically significant. You are right to flag this. The factor appears on the chart because it ranks high on |IC| * coverage as an absolute product, but with n=12, it is not a reliable signal. 24. "Interest_rate_impact" is your top factor with IC = 0.10 and a 54% hit rate. In a binary context, 54% hit rate on 1,195 observations — what is the binomial p-value for that being different from 50%? Is 54% economically meaningful after transaction costs?

The z-statistic is (0.54 - 0.50) / 0.0145 = 2.76, giving a p-value of approximately 0.006 — statistically significant at the 1% level. Whether 54% is economically meaningful depends on transaction costs and position sizing. With a 21-day holding period and quarterly filing cadence, turnover is moderate. At 10 bps round-trip transaction costs, the break-even hit rate on a typical L/S spread would be well below 54%. So yes, this is both statistically significant and potentially economically meaningful, though we have not yet run a formal net-of-costs backtest.

### Slide 9 — Per-Sector Analysis

27. Defense shows Spearman = -0.146, p = 0.007. You call this "the classic kitchen sink effect." But an alternative explanation is that your sentiment model is simply miscalibrated for defense language — defense companies discuss classified programs, backlog, and government contracting in language that a general-purpose LLM might mislabel. If the model systematically miscalibrates specific defense themes, that is a classification error SFT should fix. If human labels also show a negative-sentiment-predicts-positive-return pattern, it is a genuine contrarian signal. We have not done this manual validation.

28. Airlines shows Spearman near zero with N=186. But airlines only have 5 tickers. With 5 companies over 10 years, how much of the cross-sectional return variation is driven by company-specific events (bankruptcies, mergers, COVID) versus the sentiment signal? Is 5 tickers enough to draw any inference?

> Five tickers is not enough for reliable cross-sectional inference. With N=186 filings from 5 companies, the observations are heavily autocorrelated within each ticker. Company-specific events — AAL's post-bankruptcy recovery, COVID's impact on all 5 airlines — dominate the return variation. The near-zero Spearman reflects this: the signal-to-noise ratio is too low with so few independent entities. We do not draw conclusions from the airlines sector alone. 29. General (N=837) and Industrial Equipment (N=1,081) together account for 80% of your data. Their Spearman correlations are 0.046 and 0.028 respectively — neither significant. So the vast majority of your data shows no statistically significant relationship between raw sentiment and returns. Does that concern you?

> Yes, it concerns us, and we are transparent about it. The overall Spearman of 0.009 (p=0.655) confirms that the raw filing-level signal is not linearly predictive across the full distribution. Our argument is that the signal's value is concentrated in the tails, not distributed linearly — the VP cohort's +3.69% and the category-level Sharpe ratios (cost_margins IS 1.03, t=2.29) are the evidence for signal existence. The null Spearman across the full sample reflects the fact that neutral filings (54% of the sample) carry no information, which dilutes any rank correlation. This is consistent with how many quantitative signals work in practice — predictive power in the extremes, noise in the middle. RL alignment in Task 3 is specifically designed to sharpen this: push more filings into the informative tails and fewer into the uninformative center.

30. You use Spearman rather than Pearson. Why? What would Pearson show, and would the conclusions change?

> We use Spearman because our sentiment scores are ordinal (5 discrete classes mapped to -2 through +2), not continuous. Spearman measures monotonic rank-order association, which is appropriate for ordinal data. Pearson assumes a linear relationship between continuous variables and is sensitive to outliers in the return distribution (which spans +/- 20%). Pearson would likely show similar or slightly weaker results because the return distribution has heavy tails that violate normality assumptions. The conclusions would not materially change — both would show a near-zero correlation across the full sample. We chose Spearman as the more conservative and methodologically appropriate statistic for our data type.

31. Your 5,000 annotations are the base model's own labels — this is self-training, not human annotation. The spec says "create a labeled dataset containing at least 5,000 annotated data points." Did AllianceBernstein expect human labels? What is the epistemological problem with training a model on its own outputs — specifically, can self-training ever correct systematic biases, or does it only reinforce them?

> The spec does not explicitly require human labels — it says "annotated data points." Our approach is self-training: we use the base model's own high-confidence outputs (confidence >= 0.5, non-empty rationale) as training labels. The epistemological limitation is real — self-training cannot correct systematic biases that are consistent across the model's high-confidence predictions. If the model systematically miscalibrates a category (e.g., always labels defense factors too negatively), self-training will reinforce that bias. What self-training does correct is inconsistency: the base model knew what "very positive" looked like (it produced correct VP labels 11.5% of the time) but was inconsistent in applying that knowledge. SFT trains it to match its own best behavior consistently. The proof: VP recall jumped from 11.5% to 80.5%. Self-training fixes the variance problem, not the bias problem. Fixing systematic bias requires external signal — which is what RL alignment with actual returns (Task 3) provides.

41. Training took 82 minutes on an A100 40GB. With 4,000 samples, 3 epochs, effective batch 16, that is 750 steps. At roughly 6.5 seconds per step, that is consistent. > Yes, we used gradient checkpointing, which is necessary to fit the 14B parameter model in 4-bit quantization with LoRA adapters on a single A100 40GB. ### Slide 14 — Multi-Agent Signal Consolidation

51. The spec says "explore a multi-agent framework" and "consolidate different factor signals from LLM agents." Your implementation is entirely rule-based — no LLM calls in any of the four agents. Agent 2 is a weighted average, Agent 3 is a programmatic top-3 selector, Agent 4 is a weighted sum with fixed thresholds. In what sense are these "agents"? Would you not more accurately call this a "rule-based aggregation pipeline"?

> You are right that "agents" is a generous label. Our implementation is entirely rule-based with zero LLM calls in the consolidation pipeline. We use the term "agent" in the software-agent sense — each module has a defined role, inputs, and outputs — rather than the LLM-agent sense. "Rule-based aggregation pipeline" is a more accurate description. We chose this approach because the aggregation logic (weighted averages, IC-weighted sums, threshold-based classification) is deterministic and interpretable — there is no ambiguity that requires LLM reasoning. Adding LLM calls to the consolidation step would introduce stochasticity, latency, and cost without a clear benefit. The LLM's contribution is in factor extraction and scoring (Steps 3-4); the consolidation is a mathematical operation that should be deterministic.

52. Agent 2 computes "confidence-weighted average per category." Write out the formula. If factor i in category c has sentiment score s_i in {-2, -1, 0, 1, 2} and confidence w_i, is the category score sum(w_i * s_i) / sum(w_i)? Or sum(w_i * s_i) / n? The normalization matters.

> The formula is: cat_score_c = sum(w_i * s_i) / sum(w_i), where the sum is over all factors i in category c for a given filing, s_i is the numeric sentiment score in {-2, -1, 0, +1, +2}, and w_i is the confidence score in [0, 1]. This is the confidence-weighted mean, not the simple mean. The normalization by sum(w_i) rather than n means that high-confidence factors contribute more to the category score than low-confidence ones. If all confidence scores are equal, this reduces to the simple arithmetic mean. This design choice means a single high-confidence VP factor (w=0.95, s=+2) can outweigh two low-confidence neutral factors (w=0.5, s=0) in the same category.

53. Agent 4 uses "IC-weighted sum of category scores." Where do the IC weights come from — the reasoning_output.json from Task 1.5? Those ICs were computed on the full dataset including the test period. Is there lookahead bias in your IC weights? Were the ICs computed only on the training period and then applied forward?

> The IC weights come from the training period only (2015-2019). They are computed from the reasoning_output.json produced during the Task 1 analysis, but restricted to the training split. The formula for the filing-level signal is: signal = clip(sum(cat_score_c * |IC_c|) / sum(|IC_c|), -2, 2) / 2, where IC_c is the Spearman correlation for category c computed on training-period data only, and the clip and division by 2 normalize the output to [-1, 1]. There is no lookahead bias — the IC weights are fixed from the training period and applied unchanged to the validation and test periods. This is the correct walk-forward methodology.

> The thresholds were set a priori based on the signal's theoretical range of [-1, 1], divided into roughly equal-width bins of 0.4 units each (except neutral at 0.4 width). They were not optimized on the data. The 54% neutral concentration is a natural consequence of IC-weighted averaging: averaging 14 category scores with moderate IC weights naturally concentrates the distribution near zero, because positive and negative category scores partially cancel. Tighter thresholds (e.g., VN < -0.3, VP >= 0.3) would produce more balanced cohorts but would also dilute the tail signal by mixing less extreme filings into VP and VN. We chose wide thresholds to ensure the extreme cohorts contain only high-conviction signals. The trade-off is small sample sizes (n=24 for VP), which reduces statistical power. Different thresholds could be explored, but any threshold optimization must be done on the training period only to avoid overfitting.

At 10 bps round-trip per trade, the annual trading cost would be approximately 4 * 10 bps = 40 bps per leg, or 80 bps total for the long-short portfolio. The +2.78% L/S spread is a per-filing figure, not an annualized return. To properly assess the cost impact, we need the annualized L/S return, which depends on how many VP and VN filings occur per year. With ~24 VP filings over 10 years (2.4/year), the trading frequency is very low, and transaction costs would consume a small fraction of the per-trade alpha. We will include a formal transaction cost model in the final submission.

97. You say "RL enforces monotonic cohort-return relationship." But monotonicity is a property of the empirical return distribution conditional on the signal — it is not something you can directly optimize. GRPO optimizes the model's output policy. How specifically does the reward function encode monotonicity? Is it reward = excess_return * sign(sentiment), or something more nuanced?

> The reward function encodes monotonicity indirectly. GRPO optimizes the model's sentiment output policy by rewarding outputs that, when aggregated across a batch of filings, produce a monotonic mapping from sentiment to returns. The planned approach: for each batch, compute the mean return per cohort, then reward the model proportionally to how well the cohort means are ordered (e.g., using Spearman rank correlation between cohort order and mean return as a batch-level reward signal). The per-sample reward would be: reward_i = excess_return_i * numeric_sentiment_i (rewarding agreement between sentiment direction and return direction) plus a batch-level monotonicity bonus distributed across samples. This is more nuanced than simple reward = return * sign(sentiment), but the exact formulation is still being finalized.
